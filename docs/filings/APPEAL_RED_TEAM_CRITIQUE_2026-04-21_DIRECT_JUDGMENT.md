## Executive Assessment

The leave-to-appeal notice is materially weaker once tested against the **actual judgment** rather than against applicant-side paraphrase. The most important shift is that the record no longer contains a missing-core-document problem. We now know precisely what the court held: the order and knowledge were established, but **neither breach nor wilfulness was proved**; the respondent-side version, supported by **Mr Mphande** and **Mr Tshabalala**, was not displaced; and punitive costs were justified in light of repeated urgent litigation that could not be substantiated.

This means the appellate theory now faces a higher burden than before. It must do more than complain about *Fakie*, *Plascon-Evans*, clause 2.5, stock incidents, training incidents, witness reliance, and costs. It must show that the court's actual, fact-sensitive reasoning was wrong on the papers. That is a much more difficult task.

## Ground-by-Ground Critique

## 1. The notice now underplays the fact that the court **did** engage the contempt burden

The direct judgment records that the applicant sought criminal sanctions and therefore had to establish contempt beyond reasonable doubt. The notice tries to turn the matter into a pure legal-framework complaint, but the judgment shows the court understood the seriousness of the burden and still concluded that the evidential foundation failed.

> **Red-team point:** the appeal is vulnerable if it reframes an evidential failure as a purely doctrinal error.

## 2. The notice still does not demonstrate why the respondent version should have been rejected on paper

The direct judgment sharpens this weakness. The court did not merely default to the existence of a dispute. It identified concrete respondent-side evidentiary support — especially **Mphande** and **Tshabalala** — and held that the applicant had not proved breach or wilfulness.

> **Red-team point:** unless the appeal can produce objective contradictions, the attack on the respondent version remains under-particularized.

## 3. The stock allegations now face a direct adverse factual finding

The judgment states that the evidence did not show disruption of stocktake or delivery by the first respondent. This is more damaging to the appeal than the earlier inferred model because the appellant must now show that the court erred on a specific factual conclusion.

> **Red-team point:** an appellate complaint that merely reasserts the stock narrative without displacing the court's finding is weak.

## 4. The training allegations now look even more like contested management friction rather than contempt

The judgment reflects the respondent explanation that training supervision and certificate authority formed part of the factual context. The court did not accept that these incidents proved wilful disobedience.

> **Red-team point:** the appeal may be trying to criminalize disputed operational authority rather than demonstrate deliberate contempt.

## 5. The punitive-costs attack is materially weaker after the direct judgment

The court did not impose punitive costs in a vacuum. It recorded that the applicant had repeatedly approached the court on an urgent basis for relief that could not be substantiated and noted the first respondent's contention that contempt proceedings were being used to bolster the forfeiture claim in the divorce.

> **Red-team point:** the appeal now has to overcome a judicial-discretion decision backed by explicit contextual reasoning.

## 6. The notice remains strategically vulnerable on the void-*ab-initio* / integrity-of-order theme

The direct judgment does not eliminate the wider attack on the integrity of the 19 August 2025 order. Instead, it makes the applicant-side situation more awkward: the appellant is seeking to revive contempt on an order already attacked as materially compromised, while also having lost on proof of breach and wilfulness.

> **Red-team point:** the appeal still does not neutralize the broader integrity challenge to the underlying order.

## Updated Bottom Line

With the full judgment now integrated, the leave-to-appeal notice looks less like a strong appellate instrument and more like a **re-argument attempt after a directly reasoned factual defeat**. Its central weakness is no longer merely that it lacked a judgment anchor. Its weakness is that, once the judgment is read, the notice does **not yet demonstrate why the court was wrong to find that breach and wilfulness were unproved, or why punitive costs were an unreasonable response to repeated urgent litigation escalation**.
