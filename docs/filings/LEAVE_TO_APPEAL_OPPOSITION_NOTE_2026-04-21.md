# Opposition Note to the Application for Leave to Appeal

**Case:** 2025/137857  
**Court a quo:** High Court of South Africa, Gauteng Division, Pretoria  
**Judgment opposed:** Labuschagne J, 26 March 2026  
**Target filing opposed:** Notice of Application for Leave to Appeal dated 30 March 2026  
**Author:** Manus AI

---

## Purpose

This note consolidates the optimal respondent-side answer to the applicant's **application for leave to appeal**. It is built on three pillars: the **direct judgment** of 26 March 2026, the applicant's own **30 March 2026 notice**, and the statutory threshold in **section 17 of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**.[1] [2] [3] The note is designed to support a disciplined opposition in which the respondent-side answer remains tightly anchored to what the court actually held, rather than to post-judgment re-characterizations of the record.

## Executive Position

Leave to appeal should be opposed because the applicant has not shown that another court **would** come to a different conclusion on the papers, nor has the applicant shown any other compelling reason why the appeal should be heard.[3] [4] The court a quo expressly held that, although the existence of the order and the respondents' knowledge of it had been established, **neither breach nor wilfulness had been proved**.[1] The application thus failed on the decisive merits elements of contempt, not on a mere technicality.[1]

The appellate notice does not neutralize that core finding. Instead, it largely repeats the same themes already rejected by the court: the *Fakie* burden point, the *Plascon-Evans* complaint, a broad reading of clause 2.5, the stock and training incidents, attacks on the respondent version, and the punitive costs order.[1] [2] That is insufficient under section 17, because an arguable complaint is not enough; the question is whether there is a **realistic prospect** that another court would reach a different result.[3] [4]

---

## Statutory Threshold Under Section 17

Section 17(1)(a) of the Superior Courts Act provides that leave to appeal may be granted only if the court is of the opinion that the appeal **would** have a reasonable prospect of success, or that there is some other compelling reason why the appeal should be heard.[3] A recent High Court formulation confirms that the threshold is materially higher than a mere arguable case and that the word **"would"** imposes a more stringent standard than the older formulation.[4]

> "Leave to appeal may only be granted if the court is of the opinion that the appeal would have a reasonable prospect of success, or there is some other compelling reason why the appeal should be heard."[4]

> "The threshold for granting leave to appeal has been significantly raised; it is not enough that the case is arguable or has some remote chance of success."[4]

This matters because the applicant must do more than restate dissatisfaction with the judgment. The applicant must show that another court **would** overturn the finding that breach and wilfulness were not proved on the motion papers.[1] [3] [4]

---

## What the Court Actually Held

The opposition should begin by restoring the judgment to the center of the enquiry. The court a quo held that the applicant bore the necessary burden for contempt proceedings and that the decisive evidential failure remained the absence of proof of **breach** and **wilfulness**.[1]

> "The applicant needs to establish contempt of court beyond reasonable doubt."[1]

> "Neither the breach nor wilfulness has been established."[1]

> "In the premises the application fails with costs on a punitive scale."[1]

The judgment also records that the applicant had, on numerous occasions, approached the court on an urgent basis for relief that could not be substantiated, and that there was substance in the submission that the contempt proceedings were being used to bolster the applicant's forfeiture position in the pending divorce.[1] That reasoning directly supports resistance to the appeal on both the merits and the costs issue.[1]

## Why the Applicant Has Not Shown Reasonable Prospects of Success

The proposed appeal remains vulnerable because it attacks the judgment at the level of doctrine while failing to overcome the judgment at the level of **fact**. The court did not merely announce legal propositions. It applied them to the actual incidents relied upon by the applicant and concluded that those incidents did not establish contempt.[1] The notice, by contrast, largely repackages the same stock, training, and practical-control narrative already tested and rejected on the papers.[1] [2]

That gap is critical. An appeal court is not invited to revisit the matter because a litigant remains aggrieved. It is invited only where there is a sufficient prospect that the judgment is wrong in a way that another court would correct.[3] [4] The present notice does not reach that threshold because it does not demonstrate how the decisive finding on **breach** and **wilfulness** could realistically be overturned on the same paper record.[1] [2]

## Why There Is No Other Compelling Reason to Hear the Appeal

The notice also invokes the alternative limb of section 17, namely that there is some other compelling reason why the appeal should be heard.[2] That contention is weak on the current record. The notice does not identify conflicting authority requiring resolution, an unsettled legal issue of broader public importance, or any unusual feature making appellate intervention necessary despite the absence of reasonable prospects.[2] [3]

Instead, the filing presents what is, in substance, a **case-specific attempt to re-argue a fact-sensitive contempt dispute**. The direct judgment makes that clear. The court's reasons were contextual, evidence-sensitive, and tailored to the stock and training incidents before it.[1] That is the opposite of the kind of broader legal uncertainty usually associated with a compelling reason for appellate reconsideration.[3] [4]

---

## The Costs Issue Should Strengthen, Not Weaken, the Opposition

The punitive costs challenge does not assist the applicant. The costs order was not detached from the merits. The judgment expressly records that the applicant had repeatedly approached the court on an urgent basis for relief that could not be substantiated, and that the contempt proceedings appeared to be serving a broader litigation strategy connected to the divorce forfeiture dispute.[1] Those findings supply an articulated rationale for punitive costs.[1]

Accordingly, the respondent-side opposition should frame costs as a **chronology issue**, not a freestanding afterthought. The relevant pattern is visible in the record: the 19 August 2025 interdict, the escalation into contempt proceedings during February 2026, the dismissal on 26 March 2026, and the immediate appellate continuation on 30 March 2026.[1] [2] When that sequence is read as a whole, the costs order appears rationally linked to repeated urgent overreach rather than to any misdirection by the court.[1]

## Filing-Ready Core Submission

The following formulation should be preserved for direct use or adaptation in any respondent-side opposition:

> The applicant has failed to demonstrate that the proposed appeal would have a reasonable prospect of success as required by section 17(1)(a)(i) of the Superior Courts Act.[3] The court a quo expressly held that, while the order and knowledge thereof were established, neither breach nor wilfulness had been proved.[1] The notice for leave to appeal does not displace that decisive finding. It merely re-asserts the same stock, training, and practical-control complaints already rejected on the papers.[1] [2]
>
> Nor has the applicant demonstrated any compelling reason why the appeal should be heard under section 17(1)(a)(ii).[3] The proposed appeal raises no conflicting authority, no novel point of law, and no issue of wider jurisprudential consequence. It is a fact-sensitive attempt to revisit a failed contempt application and the punitive-costs consequences of repeated urgent litigation that could not be substantiated.[1] [4]

## Bottom Line

The application for leave to appeal should be opposed on the basis that it fails both limbs of section 17. It does not show that another court **would** come to a different conclusion on the existing record, and it identifies no separate compelling reason for appellate intervention.[3] [4] Once the direct judgment is placed at the center of the analysis, the appeal reads not as a strong appellate candidate, but as a continued attempt to re-litigate a fact-sensitive contempt dispute after the court has already found that **breach and wilfulness were not proved**.[1] [2]
