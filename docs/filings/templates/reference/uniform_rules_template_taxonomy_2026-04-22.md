**Purpose:** Map the Uniform Rules of Court into a practical filing-template taxonomy, identify which templates already exist in the repository surface, and specify the missing templates that should now be drafted.[1] [2] [3] [4]

## Purpose

This taxonomy exists to prevent the next template expansion from becoming an undifferentiated precedent dump. The Uniform Rules of Court are broad, but not every rule yields a reusable filing template. The practical objective is therefore to identify the rules that normally generate **filed procedural instruments**, group them by litigation phase, and distinguish between templates that are already covered, templates that are missing, and templates that are too context-specific to standardize responsibly.[1] [2] [3]

Under the **optimal-cognitive-grip** framework, the template library should maximize self-awareness, differentiability, composability, executability, and convergence. In practical terms, that means the library must be posture-aware, rule-aware, and sequence-aware. It must help the user choose the correct instrument for the correct stage, rather than merely increasing document volume.[2] [4]

## Taxonomy Logic

The Uniform Rules reference already groups the rules into procedural families. For template purposes, those families can be reduced into a filing-centric sequence: **service and delivery**, **applications and party structure**, **action pleadings**, **exceptions and amendments**, **irregular-step enforcement**, **judgments and interlocutories**, **discovery and trial preparation**, **withdrawal and settlement**, **rescission and review**, **execution and suspension**, **security and taxation**, **appeals**, and **special proceedings**.[3]

| Filing phase | Main rules | Template potential | Why it matters |
|---|---|---|---|
| Service and delivery | 4A, 5 | Medium | These rules often support notices and procedural applications, especially edictal citation and electronic delivery contexts. |
| Applications and representation | 6, 7, 10, 16, 16A | Very high | These rules generate many common procedural wrappers: notices of motion, authority challenges, joinder, attorney appointment, and amicus participation. |
| Action initiation and pleadings | 17–24 | Very high | These rules govern summons, declaration, intention to defend, plea, exception, strike-out, and reconvention. |
| Time, bar, amendment | 26–28 | Very high | These rules routinely produce bar notices, condonation/removal-of-bar applications, and amendment notices. |
| Irregularity enforcement | 30, 30A | Very high | These are central in contested procedural litigation and are already case-relevant here. |
| Judgments and special determination | 31–34A | High | Default judgment, summary judgment, stated case, offers to settle, and interim-payment applications often require standard forms. |
| Discovery and trial preparation | 35–38A | Very high | Discovery notices, inspection notices, expert notices, Rule 37 materials, and affidavit-evidence applications are classic template territory. |
| Withdrawal, settlement, mediation | 41, 41A | High | These frequently generate notices of withdrawal, settlement, abandonment, tender, and mediation statements. |
| Rescission and review | 42, 53 | Very high | These rules are central to order-integrity, rescission, and review architecture. |
| Execution and suspension | 45, 45A, 46, 46A | Medium-High | Not every step is a reusable template, but suspension, stay, and execution-related applications clearly are. |
| Security and taxation | 47, 48 | High | Security-for-costs notices/applications and review-of-taxation instruments are standardizable. |
| Appeals | 49–52 | Very high | Leave-to-appeal, appeal notices, cross-appeal, directions, and associated response templates are crucial. |
| Special proceedings | 54, 57, 58 | Medium | Criminal-division items, curatorship, and interpleader can be templated, but are more context-sensitive. |

## Missing Templates Identified for Drafting

The missing templates fall into two groups. The first group consists of **high-priority case-relevant templates** that connect directly to the current posture. The second group consists of **general High Court templates** that should exist for completeness because they arise frequently under the Uniform Rules.

## Rules Better Treated as Guidance Rather Than Standard Templates

Some rules do not lend themselves to clean reusable templates because they are heavily court-order-driven, operational, or evidence-specific.[3]

| Rule family | Better treatment |
|---|---|
| Pure definitional and e-justice rules | Guidance note, not standalone pleading template |
| Registrar / internal record-management rules | Workflow instructions rather than filed precedents |
| Highly technical execution rules | Decision guides plus sample relief clauses, not generic one-size-fits-all precedents |
| Criminal-division rules | Separate criminal-procedure pack rather than mixed civil library |
| Curatorship rules | Specialist pack with stronger fact-specific safeguards |

## Recommended Drafting Architecture

The expanded library should therefore be built in layers rather than in one monolithic file.

| Layer | Proposed deliverable |
|---|---|
| 1 | Comprehensive master library of reusable High Court procedural templates |
| 2 | Priority pack of case-relevant missing templates |
| 3 | Navigation index linking each template to its rule family and usual litigation stage |
| 4 | Warnings matrix stating when a template should **not** be used |

## Bottom Line

The correct answer to the user's request is not to pretend that every Uniform Rule produces a freestanding template. The correct answer is to produce a **comprehensive practical template library** for the rules that routinely generate filed instruments, while clearly marking which rules are better handled through guidance, workflow notes, or specialist packs. On that basis, the next drafting pass should focus on a master High Court template library plus an accelerated pack of the high-priority missing templates: **Rule 6 motion sequence, Rule 7, Rule 30, Rule 30A, Rule 42, Rule 27/condonation, and the broader appeal-step family**.[2] [3] [4]
