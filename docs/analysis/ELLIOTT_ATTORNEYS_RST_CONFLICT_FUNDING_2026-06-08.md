## Executive conclusion

The ABSA payment extract records repeated payments from **RegimA Skin Treatments CC** to **Elliott Attorneys** between **25 August 2025** and **15 April 2026** totalling **R478,957.93**. Rounded for court-facing financial summaries, the amount is **R478,958**. The payments are not, by themselves, conclusive proof of criminal illegality. They are, however, a material conflict-of-interest and authority anomaly because Elliott Attorneys appears in the litigation record as acting for Peter Andrew Faucitt and, in specific episodes, advancing litigation pressure against Jacqueline Faucitt while RegimA Skin Treatments CC was itself an affected entity.

The legally safest formulation is that the payments create a **prima facie need for disclosure and investigation** into corporate authority, retainer identity, proper purpose, and professional conflict checks. If the retainer was genuinely for RST’s own interests and authorised by unconflicted corporate decision-makers, payment by RST can be lawful. If RST funds were used to finance a personal or factional campaign against Jax as CEO, especially while she was forced to pay personally for her defence, the pattern supports civil claims for breach of fiduciary duty, unfair prejudice, improper-purpose expenditure, and a possible Legal Practice Council complaint.

## Legal significance

The Close Corporations Act 69 of 1984 is the direct starting point because the evidence identifies the payer as **RegimA Skin Treatments CC**. Section 42 provides that every member stands in a fiduciary relationship to the corporation, must act honestly and in good faith, must exercise powers in the interest and for the benefit of the corporation, and must avoid material conflicts between personal interests and the corporation’s interests. Section 49 provides a remedy where conduct is unfairly prejudicial, unjust, or inequitable.

The Companies Act 71 of 2008 is relevant by analogy for director-governance analysis, related entities, and forum routing. Sections 75 and 76 require disclosure of personal financial interests, proper purpose, best interests of the company, and avoidance of using position or information to benefit another person or harm the company.

The Legal Practice Council Code of Conduct is relevant to Elliott Attorneys’ professional position. The key questions are whether Elliott Attorneys’ true client was RST, Peter, Rynette, or a combination of adverse interests; whether any confidential information from RST or Jax could prejudice Jax; whether a conflict check was performed; and whether the litigation was being used to create an inequality of arms rather than to vindicate a proper corporate right.

## Evidentiary cautions

The payment schedule proves payments from RST to Elliott Attorneys. It does not yet prove the engagement letter, client identity, retainer scope, invoice descriptions, authorising person, or the subjective intent behind the retainer. Any filing should avoid overclaiming until those documents are obtained. The strongest current phrase is **“RST-funded legal-cost anomaly requiring disclosure of mandate, invoices, corporate authority, and conflict checks.”**

## Recommended next evidence requests

The filing team should request or subpoena the engagement letter, fee agreements, invoices, trust ledger, written instructions, payment approvals, board or member resolutions, conflict-check records, and all correspondence identifying who gave instructions to Elliott Attorneys. The request should also ask Elliott Attorneys to identify whether the firm regarded RST, Peter, Rynette, or another person as client for each invoice period.

## Recommended pleading language

> RegimA Skin Treatments CC paid Elliott Attorneys approximately **R478,958** between August 2025 and April 2026. The payments require scrutiny because Elliott Attorneys appears in the litigation record as acting for Peter Andrew Faucitt and advancing litigation adverse to Jacqueline Faucitt, while Jax was required to fund her own defence personally. Unless a valid, unconflicted corporate mandate is produced showing that the expenditure served RST’s own proper interests, the payments are prima facie evidence of a conflict-of-interest, improper-purpose use of corporate funds, and unfairly prejudicial conduct.
