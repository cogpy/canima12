## 1. Executive summary

The document is a **petition to the Supreme Court of Appeal** dated 1 June 2026. It is brought by **Peter Andrew Faucitt** after Labuschagne J refused leave to appeal on 4 May 2026. The petition asks the SCA for leave to appeal against the whole of Labuschagne J’s 26 March 2026 judgment and order dismissing Peter’s contempt application, and it also seeks condonation if any filing, form, pagination, or service requirement was not met.[1] The petition is expressly framed under **section 17(2)(b) of the Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013**, which allows the SCA to grant leave after the court a quo has refused it.[2]

In substance, Peter’s petition tries to revive the contempt case by arguing that the High Court wrongly interpreted paragraph 2.5 of the 19 August 2025 interim order. He says paragraph 2.5 should have been read broadly as preventing Jacqueline from involvement in stock, training, certification, employees, client relations, and business administration where prejudice may be sustained.[1] He further argues that the High Court misapplied the relationship between the **Plascon-Evans** motion-proceeding rule and the **Fakie** contempt burden-shifting structure.[1] His additional grounds are that the High Court allegedly ignored incorporated earlier papers, assessed the evidence too fragmentarily, and imposed an unjustified attorney-and-client costs order.[1]

The document does **not** directly decide or address the deeper repo-side theory that the 19 August 2025 ex parte order is void *ab initio* because it was allegedly obtained through fraud, perjury, material non-disclosure, and legally impossible banking-premise allegations.[5] That omission is strategically important. The petition assumes the August order remains valid and enforceable, whereas the existing evidence package in `revstream1` and draft Rule 42 materials in `ad-res-j7` take the position that the foundational order should be rescinded or declared void.[5] [6]

The recommended immediate response is therefore dual-track. First, oppose the SCA petition on its own terms by defending the contempt judgment: **breach and wilfulness were not established beyond reasonable doubt; the respondent’s version, supported by affidavits, at minimum raised reasonable doubt; the SCA petition is largely a fact-specific attempt to relitigate failed contempt proceedings; and the punitive-costs issue, while the applicant’s strongest appellate point, does not justify SCA intervention into the whole contempt dispute.** Secondly, use the petition as fresh evidence of ongoing reliance on the August order and accelerate a professionally refined Rule 42/common-law fraud rescission application attacking the foundation order itself.

The filing begins with a notice of motion in the Supreme Court of Appeal. The relief sought is narrowly appellate: leave to appeal against the 26 March 2026 judgment and order, condonation if required, costs in the appeal or against opposing respondents, and further or alternative relief.[1] The supporting affidavit then explains that the petition follows the High Court’s refusal of leave on 4 May 2026.[1]

Section 17(1) provides that leave to appeal may only be given where the judge or judges concerned are of the opinion that the appeal **would have a reasonable prospect of success** or that there is **some other compelling reason why the appeal should be heard**.[2] Section 17(2)(b) then allows the SCA to grant leave when the court that gave the decision has refused it, provided the application is filed within one month after refusal or within a longer period allowed on good cause.[2]

> “Leave to appeal may only be given where the judge or judges concerned are of the opinion that — (i) the appeal would have a reasonable prospect of success; or (ii) there is some other compelling reason why the appeal should be heard…” — Superior Courts Act 10 of 2013, section 17(1)(a).[2]

## 3. Core theory advanced by the applicant

Peter’s affidavit advances five main complaints. First, he says paragraph 2.5 of the August order was interpreted too narrowly. The petition emphasises the phrases **“dealing with the business and administration”**, **“employees”**, **“partners”**, **“clientele”**, **“in any manner”**, and **“may sustain any prejudice”**. The applicant’s argument is that these words are preventative and prospective; they do not require final, quantified, already-completed harm.[1]

Secondly, he says the contempt test was misapplied. In his formulation, once the existence of the order, knowledge of the order, and non-compliance were established, wilfulness and *mala fides* should have been presumed unless Jacqueline’s evidence raised a reasonable doubt. He argues that Labuschagne J acknowledged the principle but then used Plascon-Evans as if it overrode the contempt burden-shift.[1]

Thirdly, he argues that the High Court treated incidents as isolated episodes rather than as a cumulative pattern. The incidents identified include stock and product-release issues, training sessions, certification authority, employee directions, statements allegedly made to attendees, and the work phone.[1]

Fourthly, he says the High Court wrongly disregarded earlier papers that were allegedly incorporated by reference into the contempt application. The petition says those papers were relevant not just to later events but to the purpose and context of the August order.[1]

Fifthly, he challenges the attorney-and-client costs order. He says the High Court improperly treated the contempt application as an abuse designed to bolster a forfeiture claim in divorce proceedings, despite the public character of enforcing court orders.[1]

## 4. The annexed High Court judgment is central to the answer

The annexed 26 March 2026 contempt judgment records that the contempt application relied on a stocktake incident, training-session incidents, allegations about certificates, employee attendance, and the work phone.[1] Labuschagne J then records Jacqueline’s answer: she denied interference with stocktake processes; Mphande filed an affidavit supporting her version; training attendance was explained by supervision of Cherie Smith and certification authority; Farrar and Williams were described as having limited roles; and Tshabalala’s affidavit supported aspects of the training explanation.[1]

The judgment expressly states that because criminal sanctions were sought, contempt had to be established beyond reasonable doubt.[1] That approach is supported by **Fakie NO v CCII Systems**, where the SCA held that civil contempt has a criminal dimension and that committal-type relief requires proof beyond reasonable doubt.[3] Fakie also explains that contempt requires deliberate and *mala fide* disobedience, and that good-faith belief, even if mistaken, is inconsistent with contempt intent.[3]

> “A deliberate disregard is not enough, since the non-complier may genuinely, albeit mistakenly, believe him- or herself entitled to act in the way claimed to constitute the contempt. In such a case good faith avoids the infraction.” — *Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd*.[3]

This matters because the applicant’s petition repeatedly argues that Jacqueline’s admitted involvement in training, certification, and product processes should itself count against her. The stronger answer is that contempt does not arise merely because the respondent acted intentionally in a business sphere. The applicant still had to prove that the particular conduct breached a clear and enforceable restraint, and that it did so wilfully and *mala fide*. On the evidence summarised by Labuschagne J, the respondent’s explanations at least raised reasonable doubt as to breach and intent.[1] [3]

The annexed contempt judgment also records that the application failed with attorney-and-client costs. Labuschagne J reasoned that there was “much to be said” for the submission that Peter was using contempt proceedings to bolster a forfeiture claim in pending divorce proceedings.[1] That costs reasoning should be treated as a separate appellate risk: even if the contempt dismissal is robust, appellate courts are sometimes more willing to interfere with punitive costs where the reasons are insufficiently particularised. The opposition should therefore defend the costs order with direct evidence of repeated urgent proceedings, failure to substantiate relief, and litigation-pattern abuse, rather than relying only on the matrimonial context.

## 5. Procedural posture and risk assessment

The petition is procedurally significant because it attempts to move the dispute from the High Court into the SCA. It also attempts to convert a dismissed urgent contempt application into a broader appellate statement about interim business-control orders, Plascon-Evans, and civil contempt. Under section 17, however, the applicant must do more than show that another court might reason differently in the abstract. The applicant must satisfy the stricter leave standard that the appeal **would** have reasonable prospects of success, or that there is another compelling reason for it to be heard.[2]

The strongest answer is that the petition remains fact-bound. It attacks how the High Court characterised stock access, training attendance, certification authority, and employee involvement on the papers. The SCA is not ordinarily the forum for re-arguing granular affidavit disputes unless a true legal principle or clear misdirection warrants intervention. The applicant tries to create such a principle by framing the matter as a conflict between Fakie and Plascon-Evans, but the High Court’s approach can be defended as orthodox: the applicant first had to establish breach/non-compliance on admissible facts; in motion proceedings, genuine disputes were assessed on the respondent’s version; and because punitive contempt sanctions were sought, proof beyond reasonable doubt was required.[1] [3] [4]

## 6. Relationship to the void-ab-initio evidence theory

The petition assumes that the August order is valid and merely disputes its interpretation. The current evidence repository contains a materially different theory: that the August order was allegedly procured through fraud, perjury, material non-disclosure, and a legally defective banking-mandate premise, such that it should be treated as void *ab initio* or rescinded.[5]

This is strategically important for two reasons. First, if the August order is rescinded or declared void because of fraud or material non-disclosure, the foundation of the contempt theory becomes significantly weaker. Secondly, Peter’s SCA petition repeatedly insists that the August order remains practically important and still regulates business affairs. That insistence can be used to show why prompt rescission or variation is necessary: the order is not historical; it continues to be invoked as a control instrument.[1] [5] [6]

The draft Rule 42 materials in `ad-res-j7` already identify a possible rescission route and draft relief, including rescission of the 14/19 August 2025 ex parte order, referral to prosecuting authorities, and personal costs.[6] However, those materials should be refined before filing. The tone should be made strictly evidentiary and court-facing: replace conclusory language with verified document references, attach the FNB mandate and legal letter, demonstrate why each alleged non-disclosure was material to the ex parte court, and separate legal conclusions from primary facts.

## 8. Recommended response themes for the SCA petition

The response should avoid emotional language and should not overstate criminal allegations within the SCA answer unless directly relevant. The SCA petition is about leave to appeal from a contempt dismissal. The best response is therefore disciplined: **there is no appealable misdirection, no reasonable prospect, no compelling SCA-level issue, and no basis to disturb the contempt dismissal.**

A draft response should emphasise the following propositions in complete, evidence-linked paragraphs. The August order must be interpreted reasonably, not as a blanket removal of Jacqueline from every operational area. If the order were broad enough to prohibit all normal director or specialist involvement, its scope needed to be clear before quasi-criminal contempt consequences could follow. The applicant’s case depends on converting ordinary or justified conduct into contempt after the fact.

The response should also argue that Fakie does not assist Peter unless non-compliance is first established. Fakie’s burden-shift arises after order, notice/knowledge, and non-compliance are proved. Here, non-compliance was not proved beyond reasonable doubt; and the respondent’s explanations, supported by third-party affidavits, at minimum raised reasonable doubt as to wilfulness and *mala fides*.[1] [3]

On Plascon-Evans, the response should be that motion-proceeding fact rules are not displaced by contempt law. Plascon-Evans remains relevant when deciding whether the applicant has proved the factual predicates of contempt on affidavit. The applicant cannot obtain contempt sanctions by labelling respondent explanations as admissions of breach when those explanations show lawful, bona fide, or necessary operational reasons.[3] [4]

Finally, on costs, the response should defend the attorney-and-client order by showing why the contempt application was not a bona fide enforcement step but part of a repeated urgent-litigation pattern. If the evidence does not support that in sufficient detail, the fallback position should be that even if costs are reconsidered, the contempt dismissal itself should not be disturbed.

## 11. Bottom-line recommendation

The document is procedurally important but not substantively decisive. It is Peter’s attempt to obtain SCA permission to appeal a failed contempt application. Its legal centre is the interpretation of paragraph 2.5 of the August order and the alleged misapplication of contempt principles. Its strategic vulnerability is that it depends on an order whose validity is separately challenged by the evidence repository, and it attempts to convert factual and contextual disputes into SCA-level legal questions.

The recommended course is to **oppose the SCA petition promptly and technically**, with particular attention to deadlines, condonation, lack of reasonable prospects, absence of a compelling SCA issue, and the respondent’s reasonable-doubt answer under Fakie. At the same time, the team should **accelerate a refined rescission/void-ab-initio application** because that is the more structural response: it attacks the legal foundation on which the SCA petition depends. Regulatory and criminal referrals should proceed only through clean, evidence-specific bundles so that civil, regulatory, and criminal burdens are not conflated.
