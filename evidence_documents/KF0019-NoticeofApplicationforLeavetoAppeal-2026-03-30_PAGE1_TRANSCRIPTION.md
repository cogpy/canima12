**NOTICE OF APPLICATION**  
**FOR LEAVE TO APPEAL**

**TAKE NOTICE THAT,** on a date and at a time to be determined by the Registrar of the above Honourable Court, the applicant intends to apply for Leave to Appeal, to the Full Bench of the above Honourable Court, *alternatively* the Supreme Court of Appeal, against the whole of the Judgment and Order — on the grounds set out in this Notice — of the Honourable Justice Labuschagne, handed down in the above Honourable Court on 26 March 2026.

**AND TAKE NOTICE THAT** this Application for Leave to Appeal is premised on the Appeal having a reasonable prospect of success; and that there are compelling reasons why the Appeal should be heard as envisaged at Section 17 of the Superior Courts, 10 of 2013, as set out in this Notice of Application for Leave to Appeal.

**TAKE FURTHER NOTICE** that the application for Leave to Appeal is premised on, save for as aforesaid, the fact that another court would come to a different conclusion.

**AND TAKE NOTICE THAT** this Application for Leave to Appeal is brought on the following mixed grounds of law and fact, in that the Court erred, *alternatively* misdirected itself in the following respects, and for the compelling reasons set out herein:

**1.**

**1.1** By failing to grant the application.

**1.2** By dismissing the application.

**1.3** By failing to apply the correct test to contempt of Court proceedings, as set out in *Fakie NO v CCII Systems (Pty) Ltd* [2006] ZASCA 52; 2006 (4) SA 326 (SCA) at para 42.

**1.4** By failing to recognise that, once the existence of the order, knowledge thereof, and non-compliance were established, an evidential burden shifted to the Respondent to raise a reasonable doubt as to wilfulness and *mala fides*.

**1.5** By having incorrectly placed reliance on the *Plascon-Evans* test, as opposed to following the aforesaid test for contempt proceedings.

**1.6** By having, in relying on the *Plason-Evans* test, failed to find that the relief sought should be granted, in circumstances where:

> “... A final order, whether it be an interdict or some other form of relief, may be granted if those facts averred in the applicant's affidavits, which have been admitted by the respondent, together with the facts alleged by the respondent, justify such an order. The power of the court to give such final relief on the papers before is not confined to such a situation. In certain instances, the denial by respondent of a fact alleged by the applicant may not be such as to raise a real, genuine or bona fide dispute of fact. ...”[^1]

**1.7** By incorrectly requiring the Applicant to establish wilfulness on the Respondent's version.

**1.8** By having failed to find the respondent’s version to be *‘far-fetched and untenable’* and that the applicant’s version should be accepted despite the *Plascon-Evans* Test.

**1.9** By having incorrectly placed reliance on the *Plascon-Evans* test as determinative of the enquiry.

[^1]: *Plascon-Evans Paints Limited v Van Riebeeck Paints (Pty) Ltd* 1984 (3) SA 623 (A) at 635 H – I.
